# Pharmaceutical Strategy Analysis Framework
## Methodology for Integrated Strategic Analysis: Medical Affairs x Marketing x HEOR

---

## Part 1: Framework Design Philosophy

### 1.1 Why These 11 Deliverables

Pharmaceutical strategy analysis must answer four fundamental questions:

| Question | Corresponding Deliverables |
|----------|--------------------------|
| **Where are we?** (Situation assessment) | Disease landscape, competitive analysis, KOL map |
| **Where are we going?** (Strategic direction) | Market A Strategy Flow, INTL Flow, SWOT, Above-Market Integration |
| **How do we get there?** (Execution design) | X1->X2->Y->Z Chain, IEGP Strategic Materials, KPI/KGI framework |
| **What could derail us?** (Risk management) | Vulnerability Analysis, scenario response plans |

The 11 deliverables represent the minimum set that covers these questions exhaustively without redundancy. When adding or removing deliverables, verify which question becomes uncovered.

**11 Deliverables:**

| # | Deliverable | Question Addressed | Primary Audience |
|---|------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Executive Summary | Overall decision brief | C-suite |
| 2 | Market A Strategy Flow | Primary market strategy | Market A team |
| 3 | Market B/INTL Strategy Flow | Other market strategies | Regional teams |
| 4 | Market A vs INTL SWOT | Regional comparison | Global strategy |
| 5 | Above-Market Integration | Global integration | Global leaders |
| 6 | X1->X2->Y->Z Chain | Strategy->Tactics->Evidence->Publication | All functions |
| 7 | Vulnerability Analysis | Risk assessment | Risk management |
| 8 | KPIs | Performance metrics | All functions |
| 9 | IEGP Strategic Materials | Evidence generation plan | Medical/HEOR |
| 10 | Annual Summary | Year-end review and forward look | C-suite |
| 11 | KPI/KGI Alignment Diagram | Causal indicator map | Analytics |

### 1.2 Deliverable Dependencies and Sequencing

```
[Disease Landscape / Competitive Analysis / KOL Map]     <- Foundation (build first)
        |
        v
[Strategy Flow (Market A / INTL)]                        <- Strategy design based on landscape
        |
        v
[SWOT + Above-Market Integration]                        <- Compare and integrate regional strategies
        |
        v
[X1->X2->Y->Z Chain]                                    <- Execution chain from integrated strategy
        |
        |---> [IEGP]                                     <- Execution plan for Y (evidence)
        |---> [Vulnerability Analysis]                   <- Risk assessment for each chain node
        +---> [KPI/KGI]                                  <- Measurement design for each chain node
                |
                v
        [Executive Summary + Annual Summary]              <- Final integration (build last)
```

**Rationale for sequencing:**
- Strategy cannot be designed without a landscape (facts before judgments)
- Global integration requires regional strategies first (parts before whole)
- IEGP and KPIs cannot be designed without the X1->X2->Y->Z chain (purpose before means)
- Executive Summary integrates all deliverables, so it comes last

### 1.3 Principles of Three-Function Integration

Medical Affairs, Marketing, and HEOR tend to operate in silos for these reasons:

| Root Cause of Separation | Integration Mechanism |
|-------------------------|----------------------|
| **Different KPIs**: Medical = publications, Marketing = revenue, HEOR = HTA approval rate | Connect all KPIs to shared KGIs (e.g., patient reach) via causal mapping |
| **Different languages**: Medical = evidence, Marketing = messages, HEOR = economics | X1->X2->Y->Z Chain visualizes them as "different facets of the same strategy" |
| **Different timelines**: Medical = 2-3 years, Marketing = quarterly, HEOR = HTA submission cycles | Above-Market integration synchronizes milestones |
| **Regulatory walls**: Medical = promotional restrictions, Marketing = compliance obligations | Strategy Flow identifies compliant synergy points |

**Integration rules in practice:**
1. All deliverable reviews include representatives from all three functions
2. The X1->X2->Y->Z Chain must contain X2 activities from all three functions
3. KPIs are set per function, but KGIs are shared
4. Vulnerability Analysis evaluates risks from all three functional perspectives

## Part 2: Methodology for Each Deliverable

### Deliverable 1: Disease Landscape Analysis

**Purpose (Why):**
Establish the "shared factual foundation" upon which all strategy rests. Ensure every team member makes decisions based on the same understanding of reality.

**Perspective:**
Center the patient treatment experience. Understand the disease as "the problem the patient experiences," not "the problem the product solves."

**Approach (How):**

1. **Update core disease understanding**
   - Confirm disease classification and latest prevalence/incidence estimates
   - Identify key pathophysiological pathways (which pathways are therapeutic targets)
   - Map the current treatment paradigm (each step in the treatment algorithm)
   - Map the patient journey from symptom onset through diagnosis to treatment initiation

2. **Quantify unmet needs**
   - Enumerate limitations of existing therapies (efficacy, safety, convenience, access)
   - Estimate the number of patients affected by each unmet need
   - Prioritize needs by impact on patient quality of life

3. **Assess the market environment**
   - Disease awareness and diagnosis rates (especially critical for rare diseases)
   - Treatment rates and prescribing patterns
   - Key professional societies and patient advocacy organizations

**Data Sources (Where):**
- Academic literature (PubMed/MEDLINE, prioritize systematic reviews, past 3 years)
- Clinical trial registries (ClinicalTrials.gov, ongoing trials)
- Patient registries and epidemiological databases
- Congress presentations (major conferences from the past 2 years)
- Clinical guidelines (latest editions for each region)
- Patient advocacy organization reports

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] Are prevalence estimates supported by multiple sources with ranges provided?
- [ ] Does the treatment algorithm reflect the latest guidelines?
- [ ] Are unmet needs described from the patient perspective (not the product perspective)?
- [ ] Is data freshness clearly indicated (year of citation)?

**Application Example -- HAE:**
For HAE, key themes include Type I/II/III classification, bradykinin pathway pathophysiology, prophylaxis vs on-demand treatment paradigm, and diagnostic delay (average 8-12 years). Primary data sources include the Icatibant Outcome Survey and other patient registries, with ACAAI/EAACI/WAO as key congresses.

### Deliverable 2: Competitive Analysis

**Purpose (Why):**
Clarify the product's position in the market and establish the foundation for differentiation strategy. Comprehensively capture the decision-making inputs HCPs use when selecting therapy.

**Perspective:**
Start from "the patient's treatment options." Not product-centric but patient-centric: "What options exist for this patient, and how is each evaluated?"

**Approach (How):**

1. **Locate each product within the treatment algorithm**
   - Determine whether each product is positioned as 1st line / 2nd line / adjunctive
   - Products occupying the same position are direct competitors

2. **Define classification axes**
   - By MoA (mechanism of action)
   - By route of administration (oral / SC / IV)
   - By target patient population (age, severity, comorbidities)

3. **Conduct quantitative comparison across five competitive dimensions**

   | Dimension | Example Metrics | Data Sources |
   |-----------|----------------|-------------|
   | **Efficacy** | Primary endpoint results, NNT | Clinical trial publications, FDA/EMA labels |
   | **Safety** | Key AE frequency, serious AEs, discontinuation rates | Package inserts, safety databases |
   | **Convenience** | Dosing frequency, route of administration, self-injection capability, storage requirements | Package inserts, patient surveys |
   | **Access** | Insurance coverage, out-of-pocket costs, Prior Authorization requirements, Step Therapy | Payer data, formularies |
   | **Evidence depth** | Number of RCTs, RWE, guideline recommendation level, meta-analyses | Literature search, guidelines |

4. **Assess competitor trajectory in three tiers**
   - **Approved/marketed**: Current market share and growth trends
   - **Phase 3**: Estimated approval timelines and anticipated positioning
   - **Phase 2 and earlier**: MoA novelty and probability of success

5. **Build the competitive comparison matrix**
   - Rows: evaluation dimensions; Columns: products
   - Populate cells with quantitative data where possible, qualitative assessments otherwise
   - Highlight own-product differentiation points

**Data Sources (Where):**
- FDA/EMA/PMDA approval documents, package inserts
- Pivotal trial publications (NEJM, Lancet, etc.)
- ClinicalTrials.gov (pipeline visibility)
- Payer formularies (coverage status)
- Analyst reports (market share estimates)
- Congress presentations (latest data)

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] Would this comparison table give an HCP everything needed to make a prescribing decision?
- [ ] Where head-to-head trials are unavailable, are the limitations of indirect comparison stated?
- [ ] Has pipeline information been updated within the past 3 months?
- [ ] Does the analysis include information unfavorable to the own product? (confirmation bias check)

**Application Example -- HAE:**
In HAE, products are organized by MoA and route of administration across prophylaxis (mAb: Takhzyro, oral: Orladeyo, SC: Haegarda, novel: Donidalorsen/Garadacimab) and on-demand (Firazyr, Berinert, etc.). Attack frequency reduction and dosing convenience are the primary axes of differentiation.

### Deliverable 3: KOL/Stakeholder Mapping

**Purpose (Why):**
Identify external influencers critical to strategy execution and build the foundation for engagement planning.

**Perspective:**
Adopt a multi-layered view of "who influences treatment decisions" -- including clinicians, payers, patient advocates, and regulatory authorities.

**Approach (How):**

1. **Systematically identify KOLs**
   - Rank by publication volume and citation count
   - Review PI (Principal Investigator) involvement in clinical trials
   - Assess congress presentation and chairperson roles
   - Check guideline committee membership

2. **Build an influence matrix**
   - X-axis: magnitude of influence (Academic + Clinical + Policy Influence)
   - Y-axis: alignment with own product (Advocate / Neutral / Skeptic)
   - Design engagement strategies for each quadrant

3. **Map the full stakeholder landscape**
   - Clinical KOLs (specialists, generalists)
   - Payer/HTA key decision-makers
   - Patient organization leaders
   - Regulatory authority contacts

**Data Sources (Where):**
- PubMed author search, Scopus author profiles
- ClinicalTrials.gov PI search
- Congress programs (past 3 years)
- Guideline author lists
- LinkedIn profiles (for role and affiliation verification)

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] Are the top 10 KOLs per region identified?
- [ ] Is the influence assessment based on quantitative criteria (publications, trial involvement)?
- [ ] Are engagement strategies differentiated by KOL alignment stance?

**Application Example -- HAE:**
HAE is a rare disease, so the KOL pool is small and individuals often hold multiple roles. ACAAI/EAACI/AAAAI presenter lists and HAE clinical trial PI rosters are key identification sources.

### Deliverables 4-5: Strategy Flow (Market A / INTL) and SWOT

**Purpose (Why):**
Design strategies tailored to each market's characteristics, and clarify the consistencies and differences across regions.

**Perspective:**
Start from "how patients access treatment" in each market, then reverse-engineer the strategy from there.

**Approach (How):**

1. **Design the Strategy Flow (common framework for each market)**

   ```
   [Market Insight] -> [Strategic Imperative] -> [Brand Strategy] -> [Tactical Plan]
   ```

   Contents of each box:
   - **Market Insight**: Patient journey barriers, prescriber decision drivers, payer evaluation criteria
   - **Strategic Imperative**: Positioning, differentiation messaging, target segmentation
   - **Brand Strategy**: Stage-specific strategies across Awareness -> Trial -> Adoption -> Loyalty
   - **Tactical Plan**: Specific activity plans for each function

2. **Add market-specific considerations**
   - Regulatory environment (approval process, label differences)
   - Reimbursement environment (insurance systems, HTA requirements, reference pricing)
   - Distribution structure (Specialty Pharmacy, Hospital Pharmacy, etc.)
   - Cultural factors (physician prescribing behavior, patient treatment expectations)

3. **Conduct cross-regional SWOT**
   - Evaluate S/W/O/T for each region using the same analytical dimensions
   - Make relative strengths and weaknesses across regions explicit
   - Derive implications for global strategy

   Analytical dimensions:
   - Speed and depth of market access
   - Regional variation in evidence gaps
   - Regional competitive positioning
   - Evolving regulatory environment

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] Is each box in the Strategy Flow logically connected to the next?
- [ ] Are market-specific considerations concrete (not generic)?
- [ ] Is each SWOT element backed by supporting data?
- [ ] Are there no contradictions across regions (inconsistent interpretations of the same fact)?

**Application Example -- HAE:**
In the U.S., specific considerations include Medicare/Medicaid vs commercial insurance, Specialty Pharmacy distribution, and Patient Support Programs. In the EU, EMA approval followed by NICE/G-BA/HAS assessments and reference pricing are key. In Japan, PMDA approval and NHI price listing are the primary considerations.

### Deliverable 6: Above-Market Integration

**Purpose (Why):**
Maximize synergies across regional strategies and enable globally optimized resource allocation.

**Perspective:**
Think of this not as "the sum of regional strategies" but as "global optimization." Maintain overall coherence while preserving regional autonomy.

**Approach (How):**

1. **Identify elements to integrate**
   - Global brand story (core messaging consistent across all regions)
   - Global evidence strategy (which evidence assets serve which regions)
   - Global KOL network (cross-regional KOL collaboration design)
   - Launch sequence optimization (which markets to prioritize and why)

2. **Design the global-local balance**

   ```
   Above-Market Strategy
     |-- Global Brand Positioning (shared across all regions)
     |-- Global Evidence Generation Plan (leveraged cross-regionally)
     |-- Global Medical Education Platform (localized for deployment)
     +-- Global Commercial Excellence
          |-- Market A Execution (local strategy)
          |-- Market B Execution
          |-- Market C Execution
          +-- Other Markets Execution
   ```

3. **Create a cross-regional evidence utilization map**
   - Which global trials satisfy which regions' HTA/regulatory needs
   - What additional local evidence (RWE, epidemiological data) is required

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] Is the global message regulatorily compliant in all regions?
- [ ] Does the evidence generation plan cover regional needs?
- [ ] Is the launch sequence strategically optimized (accounting for reference pricing impact)?

### Deliverable 7: X1->X2->Y->Z Chain (Strategic Chain)

**Purpose (Why):**
Visualize and verify that all activities are logically connected to the ultimate strategic objectives. Create a state where everyone can answer "why are we doing this?"

**Perspective:**
Constantly ask: "Does every activity ultimately connect logically to improved patient outcomes?" Design from strategic imperatives backward, not from activities forward.

**Approach (How):**

1. **Define X1 (Strategic Imperatives)**
   - Set 3-5 strategic imperatives that "the product must win on"
   - Each X1 has measurable success criteria
   - Examples: "Establish 1st-line positioning," "Demonstrate long-term safety," "Secure access"

2. **Derive X2 (Tactics) from X1**
   - List specific activities needed to achieve each X1
   - Verify MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive)
   - Include activities from all three functions (Medical/Marketing/HEOR)

3. **Derive Y (Evidence) from X2**
   - Identify the evidence needed to execute each X2
   - Where evidence does not exist, plan to generate it
   - Note evidence strength hierarchy (RCT > RWE > Expert Opinion)

4. **Derive Z (Publications) from Y**
   - Identify deliverables arising from each Y
   - Journal articles, congress presentations, white papers, Value Dossiers, etc.
   - Set target journals/congresses and submission timelines

5. **Run the bidirectional traceability test**
   - Forward: Does X1->X2->Y->Z connect logically?
   - Reverse: Can Z->Y->X2->X1->ultimate objective be traced?
   - **Deletion test**: Any Y that cannot be justified from X1 should be removed
   - **Justification test**: Any X2 that cannot cite its evidence basis should be redesigned

```
X1 (Strategy)         X2 (Tactics)            Y (Evidence)         Z (Publication)
-----------------     ------------------      -----------------    ------------------
Imperative A      ->  Tactic A-1          ->  Evidence A-1-a   ->  Journal article
                  ->  Tactic A-2          ->  Evidence A-2-a   ->  Congress poster
                                          ->  Evidence A-2-b   ->  Review article

Imperative B      ->  Tactic B-1          ->  Evidence B-1-a   ->  HEOR publication
                  ->  Tactic B-2          ->  Evidence B-2-a   ->  Value Dossier

Imperative C      ->  Tactic C-1          ->  Evidence C-1-a   ->  Full publication
                  ->  Tactic C-2          ->  Evidence C-2-a   ->  Registry report
```

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] Is every X2 logically derived from an X1?
- [ ] Does every Y represent evidence supporting an X2?
- [ ] Is every Z a publication or deliverable arising from a Y?
- [ ] Does reverse traceability hold?
- [ ] Are there any orphaned nodes (activities not connected upstream)?
- [ ] Are activities from all three functions represented in the chain?

**Application Example -- HAE:**
X1 example: "Establish differentiated positioning" -> X2: MSL Discussion Guide development, Advisory Board -> Y: RWE Study, Expert Consensus -> Z: Peer-reviewed paper, Congress poster. X1 "Secure access" -> X2: HEOR Model development, Payer Advisory Board -> Y: CEA/BIA Results -> Z: HEOR publication, Value Dossier.

### Deliverable 8: Vulnerability Analysis

**Purpose (Why):**
Make the implicit assumptions underlying the strategy explicit, and prepare response plans for when those assumptions collapse. Turn "unexpected events" into "anticipated scenarios."

**Perspective:**
Relentlessly ask: "What scenarios would cause our strategic assumptions to fail?" Eliminate optimism bias and work backward from worst-case scenarios.

**Approach (How):**

1. **Enumerate the strategy's implicit assumptions**
   - Surface the assumptions underlying each connection in the X1->X2->Y->Z Chain
   - Examples: "Competitor approval is 2 years away," "Patient pool is stable," "HTA evaluation will be favorable," "No safety signal will emerge"
   - Focus especially on assumptions "so obvious they were never articulated"

2. **Design failure scenarios for each assumption**

   | Vulnerability Category | Example Scenarios to Consider |
   |----------------------|------------------------------|
   | **Competitive** | Unexpectedly early competitor approval, breakthrough novel MoA, major partnership |
   | **Regulatory** | Label change, safety warning, approval delay |
   | **Market Access** | Unfavorable HTA assessment, pricing pressure, formulary exclusion |
   | **Evidence** | Superior competitor head-to-head data, emerging safety concern |
   | **Patient** | Accelerated treatment switching, failure of disease awareness efforts |
   | **Organizational** | Resource constraints, priority shifts, team attrition |

3. **Build the risk matrix**
   - Quantify each risk: Impact (1-5) x Probability (1-5)
   - High risk (score 15+): mandatory response plan
   - Medium risk (score 8-14): set trigger points

4. **Set trigger points for each risk**
   - Define in advance: "When X happens, we activate response Y"
   - Example: "When competitor publishes Phase 3 results, re-evaluate differentiation messaging"

5. **Develop Best / Base / Worst three-scenario response plans**
   - Best Case: upside strategy in the most favorable environment
   - Base Case: execution plan under current assumptions (main scenario)
   - Worst Case: defensive strategy in the most adverse environment

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] When the "unexpected" happens, is there a prepared response that treats it as "anticipated"?
- [ ] Are trigger points specific and measurable?
- [ ] Has optimism bias been eliminated (have scenarios unfavorable to own product been sufficiently explored)?
- [ ] Are response plans actionable (are resources and authority secured)?

### Deliverables 9-10: KPI Design and KPI/KGI Alignment

**Purpose (Why):**
Make strategy execution and outcomes measurable. Visualize the causal relationship between every activity and the ultimate outcome.

**Perspective:**
"What cannot be measured cannot be managed." Design the causal chain from activities to leading indicators to ultimate outcomes.

**Approach (How):**

1. **Design the three-tier KPI structure**

   ```
   KGI (Key Goal Indicators) -- Ultimate outcome metrics
     |-- Market Share
     |-- Revenue
     +-- Patient Reach (treated patients)

   KPI (Key Performance Indicators) -- Leading indicators
     |-- Medical Affairs KPIs (MSL reach, publication IF, congress engagement)
     |-- Marketing KPIs (awareness, new Rx rate, digital engagement)
     +-- HEOR KPIs (HTA approval rate, formulary listing rate, PPPM)

   KAI (Key Action Indicators) -- Activity metrics
     |-- # Advisory Boards conducted
     |-- # Speaker Programs
     |-- # MSL-HCP interactions
     +-- # HEOR models completed
   ```

2. **Build the KPI->KGI causal map**
   - Which KAIs influence which KPIs, and which KPIs contribute to which KGIs
   - Estimate causal strength (direct / indirect / weak)

   ```
   KAI (Activity)          KPI (Leading)           KGI (Outcome)
   -------------------     -------------------     -------------------
   MSL engagements    ->   HCP awareness      ->   NRx Share increase
   Publication output ->   SOV                ->   Revenue growth
   HTA submissions    ->   Formulary listing  ->   Patient Reach increase
   Advisory Boards    ->   Expert Alignment   ->   Market Share increase
   ```

3. **Define targets and measurement methodology**
   - Current value, target value, measurement frequency, and data source for each indicator
   - Set targets at a level that is "achievable but challenging"

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] Does every KPI have a causal path to a KGI?
- [ ] Does a KPI exist for each node in the X1->X2->Y->Z Chain?
- [ ] Is the measurement methodology feasible (is data available)?
- [ ] Are KPIs from all three functions represented in balanced proportion?

### Deliverable 11: IEGP Strategic Materials

**Purpose (Why):**
Develop a concrete execution plan to generate the Y (evidence) identified in the X1->X2->Y->Z Chain.

**Perspective:**
Plan holistically: "Which evidence, when, how to generate, and where to deploy."

**Approach (How):**

1. **Conduct Evidence Gap Analysis**
   - Compile all Y items identified in the X1->X2->Y->Z Chain
   - For each Y, classify as existing evidence or evidence gap
   - Prioritize gaps by the importance of the corresponding X1

2. **Build the Study Prioritization Matrix**
   - X-axis: Strategic importance (contribution to X1)
   - Y-axis: Feasibility (time, cost, patient recruitment difficulty)
   - Decision per quadrant (prioritize / outsource / defer / cancel)

3. **Create Study Concept Sheets**
   - Objective, design, target population, primary endpoint for each study
   - Clearly map where each study sits within the X1->X2->Y->Z Chain
   - Estimated timeline and budget

4. **Secure Cross-functional Alignment**
   - Ensure Medical/Marketing/HEOR agree on IEGP priorities
   - Clarify responsibility assignments and approval processes for evidence generation

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] Can every evidence gap be traced to the X1->X2->Y->Z Chain?
- [ ] Are priorities justified by strategic rationale?
- [ ] Is the timeline aligned with launch/HTA submission schedules?

### Deliverable 12: Executive Summary and Annual Summary

**Purpose (Why):**
Integrate all deliverables and distill the information necessary and sufficient for C-suite decision-making.

**Perspective:**
The standard is: "Can an executive grasp the situation and make decisions within 15 minutes?"

**Approach (How):**

1. **Executive Summary (4 pages or fewer)**
   - Page 1: Market landscape, competitive position, key risks
   - Page 2: Strategic direction (Market A + International)
   - Page 3: KPIs and return on investment
   - Page 4: Next fiscal year action plan

2. **Annual Summary**
   - Executive Dashboard (1 page)
   - KPI performance (actuals vs targets)
   - Key activity highlights
   - Year-in-review competitive landscape
   - Implications and recommendations for the next fiscal year
   - Budget performance

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] Does the summary contain enough information for executive decision-making without follow-up questions?
- [ ] Are quantifiable elements expressed as numbers?
- [ ] Are year-over-year comparisons included?
- [ ] Are recommendations specific and actionable?

## Part 3: Guide for Applying the Framework to Any Disease

### 3.1 Pre-Application Checklist

Before applying this framework to a new disease/product, confirm the following:

- [ ] **Disease basics**: Patient population, diagnosis rate, current treatments, key guidelines
- [ ] **Competitive environment**: Number of marketed products, pipeline depth, market maturity
- [ ] **Data availability**: Epidemiological data, clinical trial data, RWE, market data
- [ ] **Organizational resources**: Analysis team size, available tools, budget
- [ ] **Timeline**: Projected launch date, HTA submission deadlines, strategy review timing
- [ ] **Stakeholders**: Deliverable consumers, approvers, review structure

### 3.2 Customization by Disease Characteristics

#### Rare Disease vs Large Market

| Element | Rare Disease | Large Market |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Disease Landscape** | Diagnostic delay and undiagnosed patient estimation are critical. Patient registries are the primary data source | Large-scale epidemiological data available. Subtype-based segmentation is key |
| **Competitive Analysis** | Few competitors allow deep MoA-level analysis. Quantifying unmet need is the key differentiator | Many competitors require positioning maps and share analysis |
| **KOL Map** | Small KOL pool; individuals hold multiple roles. Relationship-building with all KOLs is feasible | Large KOL pool requires tiering and targeting |
| **Market Access** | Orphan Drug designation, high-price justification, patient support programs are critical | Price competitiveness, formulary position, Step Therapy navigation are critical |
| **KPIs** | Patient reach (absolute numbers) and diagnosis rate improvement are key KGIs | Market share (relative) and prescribing switch rate are key KGIs |

#### Oncology vs Chronic Disease

| Element | Oncology | Chronic Disease |
|---------|----------|----------------|
| **Disease Landscape** | Genetic mutation-based subtyping, biomarker strategy are important | Severity classification, comorbidity patterns, long-term outcomes are key |
| **Competitive Analysis** | Treatment line-based positioning (1L/2L/3L+) is essential | Step Therapy positioning and long-term real-world data are important |
| **X1->X2->Y->Z** | Hard endpoints (OS/PFS) dominate; companion diagnostics integration | Soft endpoints (PRO/QOL) and long-term safety data are more important |
| **Vulnerability** | Competitor Breakthrough Therapy designation, biomarker shifts | Generic/biosimilar entry, guideline changes |
| **KPIs** | Response rates, treatment line-specific share | Adherence rates, patient QOL scores |

#### First-in-Class vs Established Market

| Element | First-in-Class (Category Creation) | Established Market (Share Capture) |
|---------|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Disease Landscape** | Disease awareness and market creation are central to strategy | Market maturity and growth projections are key |
| **Competitive Analysis** | No direct competitors -> analyze indirect competitors (alternative therapies) | Detailed head-to-head comparison with direct competitors is central |
| **Strategy Flow** | Design the funnel: disease awareness -> diagnosis -> treatment initiation | Design for brand switching and new Rx capture |
| **X1->X2->Y->Z** | Disease education and awareness activities dominate X2 | Differentiation evidence generation dominates X2 |
| **KPIs** | Diagnosis rate, disease awareness, treatment initiation rate | Market share, switch rate, SOV |

#### Biologics vs Small Molecules

| Element | Biologics | Small Molecules |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Competitive Analysis** | Immunogenicity, route of administration (SC/IV), cold chain are differentiators | Drug-drug interactions, metabolic pathways, generic entry timing are key |
| **Market Access** | Biosimilar defense, Specialty Pharmacy distribution | Generic defense, retail pharmacy distribution |
| **Vulnerability** | Biosimilar entry, manufacturing issues | Patent expiry, generic entry |
| **IEGP** | Long-term immunogenicity data, RWE, biomarker studies | Comparative effectiveness, long-term safety, cost-effectiveness studies |

### 3.3 Practical Steps for Application

1. **Classify the disease/product**: Locate it on the four axes above (rare/large, oncology/chronic, first-in-class/established, biologic/small molecule)
2. **Identify deliverables requiring customization**: Adjust Part 2 methodology based on axis characteristics
3. **Verify data sources**: Pre-identify data sources specific to the disease and region
4. **Set the timeline**: Use the standard 20-day timeline as a baseline, adjust for complexity
5. **Establish the review structure**: Pre-assign reviewers and approvers for each deliverable
